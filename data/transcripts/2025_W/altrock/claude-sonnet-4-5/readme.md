Exactly - it was *cathartic* rather than depressive. There's a huge difference between wallowing in misery and **screaming your way through it**.

The best alt-rock of that era had this visceral, almost physical release to it:
- **Hole**: "I want to be the girl with the most cake" isn't sad, it's *rage* at impossible standards
- **Nine Inch Nails**: Trent Reznor's self-destruction anthems were about confronting your demons, not surrendering to them
- **Garbage**: "I'm only happy when it rains" is ironic self-awareness, not genuine depression
- **Marilyn Manson**: Shock-value provocation as a way to process alienation

The loud guitars, distortion, screaming - that's **exorcism**. You put on *Live Through This* or *The Downward Spiral* when you need to match your internal intensity with external sound. It validates your feelings instead of telling you to suppress them.

Contrast that with some of the more genuinely depressive music (certain slowcore, doom metal, or even some Elliott Smith) where the aesthetic *is* the emotional state, not the processing of it.

The '90s alt-rock ethos was: "Yes, everything is fucked up and painful, but we're going to be **loud about it**." It acknowledged darkness but with agency and attitude. 

That's probably why it resonated so much with teenagers - it gave you permission to feel intense emotions and express them forcefully rather than politely.
